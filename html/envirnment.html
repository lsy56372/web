Globally, habitat fragmentation driven by land use intensification, threatens biodiversity and reduces essential ecosystems services. The decreasing ability of ecosystems to provide society with services, like health benefits, is a process increasingly being described as environmental deprivation. Within Scotland, the decline of habitat connectivity governed by fragmentation is one of seven core pressures on biodiversity (Scottish Government, 2015).

11% of species are threatened with extinction (Burns et al., 2023).

In reaction to these pressures, Biodiversity Net Gain (BNG) policy mandates that new developments increase biodiversity by at least 10% through habitat enhancement (Martinez-Cillero et al., 2023). Nevertheless, guidance on where improvements need to be implemented is overall poor and lacking, often meaning environmentally deprived areas are bypassed (Martinez-Cillero, 2022). In our project's context, environmental deprivation is specifically defined as:
"Areas of low connectivity that provide less or lower ecosystem services than areas of high connectivity"

Model of environmental deprivation in Edinburgh, produced by comparing the density of connectivity and population within data zones. The inset map focuses on the city centre, where high population density and low connectivity represent a region of high relative environmental deprivation.

Connectivity modelling is important for identifying where targeted connectivity enhancement would bring environmentally deprived areas valuable ecosystem services. There exists a current paucity of research dedicated to habitat connectivity at local scales. Our project addresses this research gap by modelling habitat connectivity for targeted conservation in Edinburgh, where, biodiversity action plans, coherent with national policy, call for connectivity enhancement between fragmented habitats (City of Edinburgh Council, CEC, 2022) with assessment of environmental deprivation.

To meaningfully model and identify areas of environmental deprivation, population density within Edinburgh's data zones and density of mean connectivity were compared. This model revealed the city centre, combining its low habitat connectivity and high population density, as environmentally deprived. As a result, we declared that these areas should be prioritised for targeted conservation efforts more than areas of sparse population (e.g., industrial areas), as intervention in the former sites will bring ecosystem services to more people (Martinez-Cillero et al., 2023).

Simulation illustrating potential habitat connectivity improvements in more environmentally deprived areas, showing (a) species-averaged connectivity under ‘baseline’ unmodified land cover, (b) species-averaged connectivity under ‘improved’ land cover, artificially modified through conversion of (c) vacant land parcels, obtained through Digimap from the 2022 Scottish Vacant and Derelict Land Survey, into greenspaces that constitute a mix of grassland and woodland; (d) shows the subtraction of (a) from (b), highlighting change in connectivity between the two scenarios.

×

We modelled potential, improved habitat connectivity from Edinburgh Castle to The Meadows. Areas of vacant land were converted into greenspaces and as a result substantially improved connectivity in the city centre, creating a continuous connection between previously fragmented greenspaces, from Edinburgh Castle to The Meadows. The overall goal of this example was to visually simulate the impact of targeted conservation actions like greenspace creation on habitat connectivity and ecosystem services (CEC, 2022) in Edinburgh city centre, an area we confirmed as environmentally deprived.
